# Prologue

Have you ever wondered what **“nothing”** really is?  

It seems like everything in the world has already been named, that everything has meaning. So what is this tiny thing called **“nothing”**? Is it just the hole in a bagel, or something more profound?  

> Once, the creator of SQL, Donald D. Chamberlin, admitted that his biggest mistake was introducing **NULL** into the language.  
> Why? Because **NULL was supposed to represent “unknown” or “missing” data**, but it quickly became a source of confusion, bugs, and unexpected behavior. It broke logic in ways no one predicted—turning “nothing” into something frustratingly complicated.  

Everyone in this life wants to become someone, to believe in something, to have something—**but no one wants the orphan called “nothing.”**  

Yet, would the world exist without it? Scientists have dressed this word up with a magical name—**“dark matter”**—and dedicate their lives to studying the mystery of this emptiness.  

> It would be deeply ironic if the essence of our existence turned out to be nothing more than that enigmatic **“nothing.”**  

And what if people lived forever—would **“nothing”** still exist? Does anything have meaning if it cannot simply be erased?  

Like a pencil on an ugly sketch, or files left abandoned on a computer.  

God made the Universe out of nothing, and we are obsessed with finding the tiniest fragments of that nothing—trying to understand **how**.  

> Oh, what a task He left us!  
> You might as well invent a new religion than spend eternity laboring in science with no result.  

I don’t know how many philosophers before me have pondered this, but lately this question hasn’t let me go.  

I decided to describe my own vision of it in an **artistic, fictional style**.  

Who knows… maybe my thoughts will **resonate in your hearts**, and you will enjoy what I have written.
This book continues my reflections after my first novella about Heaven and Hell.  

I won’t say it became wildly popular, but its modest reception inspired me to start writing a second book. I simply enjoy sitting at a table with a cup of coffee and letting my imagination wander. And if pressing keys while doing that can create another masterpiece of world literature, why not?  

I won’t be like Tolstoy, boring readers with 1,000+ pages of rambling nonsense. Those who have read the first book will understand the relief in that. And who knows—if this counts as a masterpiece, perhaps I have a chance at worldwide recognition as well.  

In the immortal world I created in my imagination and then embodied in the short novella *“Man of Dawn”*, my friend Grisha began to explore the same questions.  

For those who haven’t read my first divine comedy, here’s a brief recap: the world we will travel through in this book is an imaginary world where, in 1956, humans didn’t land in outer space—they landed **in the sky**. Later, they punched a hole down to Hell and began settling these divine realms.  

In the previous book, I showed how humans corrupted this paradise and turned it into yet another earthly misery. That novella only described the world superficially.  

In this new work, I want to explore it **more deeply**, and I will be aided by two main characters:  

- **Grisha**, who has been living in Hell for nearly 100 years.  
- **Oksana**, who was born in Heaven and has never seen Earth or Hell.  

They are roughly the same age, yet they have **completely different perspectives** on the world.  

Grigory was born in the Ukrainian SSR in 1931. As a child, he barely survived the Holodomor in Donbas…
Later, he witnessed the atrocities of the Second World War. His father was killed during the conflict. Eventually, Grigory himself took up arms, joining the Ukrainian Insurgent Army, though it was already in decline. He was captured by the Soviets and sent to Siberia, where he eventually built a new life. He married, managed a large estate, and became successful—but in the Soviet Union, when you achieve success, the Party eventually comes for everything…  

Sadly, that was Grigory’s fate. He, his wife, and his daughter were killed by a KGB officer. Yet Grigory managed to bring their souls with him. One might think they would reunite in the afterlife, but Hell divided them, preventing Grisha from exacting revenge on the killer. Grisha ended up on the **third level**, while the NKVD officer was placed on the **eighth**, where he endured eternal torment (believe me, you need very good connections to survive on those levels). Still, this could not fill the void of hatred in Grigory’s heart. The only consolation was that his wife and daughter were in Heaven.  

Oksana, on the other hand, had never seen any world other than Paradise. She was born to human parents **already in Heaven**. Remember, I mentioned that humans in Heaven do not age and remain forever at the age they arrived. However, this rule does **not** apply to those who enter Heaven alive, by human means rather than divine intervention. Trust me—I meticulously follow the rules of the game I created in my mind. You will not find inconsistencies.  

Oksana was the first generation born in Heaven, so the rules of divine morality were a bit simpler for her. 
Her parents had arrived in Heaven as missionaries—back when life on Earth couldn’t be bought. From a young age, Oksana observed how humans behaved in Heaven: how they introduced new forms of morality, the absurd courts, and the enforcers of order they imposed. One could say she witnessed the **decline of Paradise** and the rise of what they now call **Prolisk**.  

Once, back when newcomers had not yet realized this was Paradise and not some Columbus-style America, a newly arrived man tried to assault her. But in Heaven, it is impossible to harm anyone. Such acts are immediately punished by divine force, and he literally **burned and was sent straight to the cruiser “Moskva.”**  

Yet the incident made Oksana reflect: how little she truly understood humans. Being born in Heaven as a human, she had no way to grasp their nature, which always bored and frustrated her. One day, she **escaped** (humans did not allow heavenly-born beings to leave Paradise because it could have caused chaos across the three worlds), paying the price for her freedom. Later, she even became the **first to visit Hell from Heaven**.  

I will not reveal all the secrets of their adventures. Ahead of us lies roughly **three hundred pages of story**. I hope you enjoy it. As for me, I’ll sit here and listen as you read. Perhaps one day my manuscripts will fall into the right hands, and I will become known posthumously. Yet by then, it will all be **“nothing”** for me.  

So, **catch the moment…**
